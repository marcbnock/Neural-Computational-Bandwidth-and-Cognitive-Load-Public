## Overview

This repository models the brain as a **predictive information processor** operating under finite metabolic and computational constraints. By integrating systems biology, neuroscience, information theory, and computational physiology, we investigate how chronic biological noise increases cognitive load, reduces signal fidelity, and contributes to burnout, brain fog, and adaptive exhaustion.

**Core Hypothesis**: Cognitive performance is fundamentally limited by the brain's capacity to process, filter, and respond to biological signals. When structural stress, autonomic dysregulation, or trigeminal noise increases, the system reallocates finite neural bandwidth from executive function to compensatory processing—creating measurable deficits in attention, decision-making, and resilience.

## The Brain as a Predictive System

The brain continuously generates predictions about incoming sensory data, comparing expected vs. actual signals to minimize prediction error. This framework investigates:

- **Predictive processing hierarchies**: How the brain allocates attention and computational resources
- **Free energy minimization**: Metabolic efficiency in information processing
- **Precision-weighted prediction errors**: How biological noise degrades signal reliability
- **Active inference**: The brain's attempts to control its sensory input through action

When chronic biological stressors (structural misalignment, airway restriction, trigeminal hyperactivity) persist, the prediction machinery must constantly correct for noisy or unexpected signals—consuming bandwidth that would otherwise support higher-order cognition.

## Biological Noise and Cognitive Load

**Biological noise** refers to persistent, low-level signals that demand ongoing neural resources without serving adaptive functions:

- Chronic trigeminal activation from structural stress
- Autonomic dysregulation from airway restriction
- Inflammatory signals from tissue strain
- Proprioceptive mismatch from postural compensation

This noise increases **cognitive load**—the total information-processing demand placed on working memory and executive systems. When biological bandwidth is consumed by noise management, fewer resources remain for:

- Complex problem-solving
- Emotional regulation
- Creative thinking
- Strategic planning
- Social cognition
